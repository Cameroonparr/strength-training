Deadlift

Conventional Deadlift

Muscles worked

The conventional deadlift is one of the most beneficial yet often misunderstood lifts in strength training. It effectively works the legs, with a particular emphasis on the hamstrings, more so than squats. In addition to targeting the lower body, deadlifts engage the core, lower back, upper back, and forearms, making it an incredibly time-efficient exercise to include in your routine. However, proper form is essential, as improper technique—especially with heavy weights—can increase the risk of injury, particularly to the lower back.

Starting position

To begin, place your hands roughly shoulder width apart, bend over and grip the bar. Hand placement should be shoulder width apart. Your feet should be about hip-width apart and pointed slightly outwards.

Lifting the bar

Prepare to lift the bar by engaging your core and lats, then begin the ascent with a controlled pull. As you lift, squeeze your glutes and drive your hips forward, keeping the bar as close as possible to your shins. At the top of the movement, ensure that your legs, hips, and shoulders are fully aligned, achieving a proper "lockout." In other words, stand up straight with your body in one strong, stable line.

Lowering the bar

As you lower the bar, return the bar to the ground in a contolled manner, not to fast and not too slow. You can achieve this by gradually releasing your glutes. The bar should be kept close to your shins and follow the same path as when you were lifting it.

Repeat!

As with the other lifts, perform as many repetitions as needed! Remember that slow, controlled movements are essential, especially with deadlifts, due to the force exerted on the spine. Focusing on control not only improves strength but also reduces the risk of injury.
